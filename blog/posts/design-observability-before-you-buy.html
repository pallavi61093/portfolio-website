How to Design Observability Before You Buy It

Most observability initiatives start with a demo. A vendor shows a beautiful dashboard, the room nods, a purchase order follows — and six months later the tool is installed but the questions it was meant to answer are still unanswered.

The tool should be the last decision you make, not the first. Here's the order I recommend instead.

1. Start with the questions

Observability isn't about collecting data; it's about answering questions under pressure. Before evaluating any platform, write down the questions your teams actually ask during an incident: Is the customer affected? What changed? Where is the latency coming from?

You don't have an observability problem — you have a question problem.

2. Map questions to signals

Only once the questions are clear can you decide which signals — metrics, logs, traces, events — you genuinely need, and at what cardinality and retention. This is where cost and value get decided, long before a contract is signed.

3. Then, and only then, choose the tool

With questions and signals defined, tool selection becomes an objective exercise: which platform answers our real questions, at our scale, for a cost we can defend? The demo stops being a sales pitch and starts being a test.
